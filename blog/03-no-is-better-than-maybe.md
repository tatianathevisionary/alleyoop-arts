# No is better than maybe

On June 1st, Canada Basketball announced its roster direction for the road to LA28, and two things happened at once. Shai Gilgeous-Alexander said yes. Jamal Murray said no.

The program's response to the no was the most Canadian basketball sentence of the year: *"No is better than maybe."*

## The doctrine

Under its new head coach, Canada Basketball has drawn a line: full commitment or none. Not "we'll see how the body feels in July." Not "pencil me in." A clean yes or a clean no, because a program can build around either — and can build around nothing in between.

Murray gave a clean no. He's out for the World Cup qualifiers and out for Los Angeles. And the program publicly respected it, because the no does something a maybe never can: it tells everyone else exactly where the floor is.

## Why the maybe is the enemy

Anyone who's run a team — a roster, a project, a business — knows the maybe is the most expensive answer in the building. The maybe holds a spot it might not fill. The maybe makes three other people plan two versions of the summer. The maybe feels kind in the moment and costs everyone for months.

A no hurts for a day. Then everyone adjusts and the floor gets solid again.

There's a version of this in every basketball life. The transfer you commit to or don't. The rehab you're all the way in on or not. And the biggest one, the one nobody practices for: the decision to stop playing. The players who walk away clean — who give the game a no instead of a decade of maybe — tend to be the ones who find their way back to it on better terms.

## What it costs

Let's not pretend the doctrine is free. Murray is one of the best players this country has produced, and a Canada roster without him is a worse roster on paper. Choosing the clean no over the talented maybe is a real bet — that culture compounds and talent can be replaced, not the reverse.

That bet is now on the record, with a date on it. LA is in 2028. We'll know.

But as a principle for a program that spent decades assembling all-star names that never quite became a team: the line is right. You cannot run an alley-oop with someone who might be there when the ball comes down.

Yes or no. The lob requires an answer.
