# The quote that was itself a pass

You've seen the quote. It's on posters, in captions, in every "basketball wisdom" thread ever assembled:

> "An assist makes two people happy. A point makes one guy happy." — Nikola Jokić

It's a perfect quote. It's also wrong twice.

## What he actually said

February 2017. Jokić is 21, in his second NBA season, and a reporter asks whether he prefers to score or to pass. His answer, as reported at the time:

> "Passing makes two people happy. Scoring only makes one person happy."

Not "an assist." Not "a point." The internet version is a paraphrase that got photocopied until everyone forgot there was an original. That's the first wrong.

The second is better.

## He was quoting Magic

Jokić didn't present the line as his own. He reached back for an old Magic Johnson idea and passed it forward — a 21-year-old Serbian center borrowing from the greatest passer who ever lived to explain why he'd rather throw the lob than finish it.

Sit with that. The most famous quote about passing is itself a pass. Magic threw it decades ago. Jokić caught it, in his second language, and finished. The internet then cropped both names down to one and changed the words.

Here's the part that keeps it honest: we went looking for Magic's original, and we couldn't verify it. The line attributed to him — "assists is what Earvin is all about" — exists only on quote-aggregator sites. No interview, no transcript, no book page. What's verified is the moment of the handover: Jokić, on the record, crediting Magic. The receipt is the pass itself.

## Why we're starting here

This is the first entry in a column we're calling The Assist Files. One quote at a time, traced back to the moment it was actually said — the date, the room, the question that prompted it. Where the trail goes cold, we say so.

Everyone aggregates quotes. Almost nobody traces them. We checked 29 of basketball's most shared lines this month and roughly two out of three couldn't be traced to a primary source. (That story is coming — it's worse and funnier than you think.)

The game deserves more than recycled captions. It deserves provenance — the same thing we'd demand of any art.

An assist only counts if you know who threw it.
