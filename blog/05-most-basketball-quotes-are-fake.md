# Most basketball quotes are fake. We checked.

We pulled 29 of the most shared basketball quotes on the internet — the ones on posters, in graduation speeches, tattooed on forearms — and tried to trace each one to a primary source. An interview with a date. A press conference with a transcript. A book with a page number.

Ten survived.

The other nineteen live exclusively on quote-aggregator sites, copied from each other in a circle, with no original anywhere. Some are paraphrases that drifted. Some belong to different people. At least one appears to be invented outright. Here's the field report.

## The ad copy wearing a jersey

**"My belief is stronger than your doubt." — Dwyane Wade.** Except Wade never said it. It's the tagline of a 2008 Converse campaign for his signature shoe — written in the third person: *"His belief is stronger than your doubt."* Somewhere along the way the internet moved it into Wade's mouth and the first person. It's a good line. A copywriter wrote it.

It has company. Michael Jordan's "I've missed more than 9,000 shots" — the most quoted basketball lines ever — is the verbatim narration of a 1997 Nike commercial. The sentiment may be all Jordan; the sentences are Wieden+Kennedy. And Kyrie Irving's beloved "basketball is an art form" riff? Scripted dialogue from his Uncle Drew soda commercials, delivered in character, in old-man makeup.

## The quotes nobody said

**"Shooters shoot." — Reggie Miller.** We could find no interview, broadcast, or book connecting the phrase to Miller. It's a folk saying — basketball's "it is what it is" — that aggregators assigned to the most famous shooter they could think of.

**"I always tried so hard to fit in, and then I figured out that I didn't want to fit." — Anthony Davis.** No source. No date. No interview. We're not even certain which Anthony Davis it's supposed to be — the NBA player or the Pulitzer-winning composer. Neither can be connected to it. Our best guess: someone made it up, and the internet shrugged and printed it.

**"Be more concerned with your character than your reputation." — John Wooden.** Wooden really did say it — it's in his books. But he didn't coin it. He credited the idea to his father, and versions of the antithesis circulate from as far back as Thomas Paine's era. Wooden was passing it down, not writing it. The aggregators cut the lineage and kept the famous name. Pattern's familiar by now.

## The ones that survived

The verified ten are a good list — Damian Lillard on what pressure actually is (Sporting News, 2017, and the full version is sharper than the cropped one), Giannis Antetokounmpo's "steps to success" press conference (April 2023, on camera), Pat Summitt's Definite Dozen (a real book, 1998, one rule per chapter).

And the best of them is the subject of [our previous letter](02-the-quote-that-was-itself-a-pass.md): Jokić's passing line, which turned out to be misquoted AND borrowed from Magic Johnson — a quote about assists that was itself an assist.

## Why an art brand is fact-checking captions

Because provenance is the whole difference between art and a poster.

When we put a sentence on a print, someone is going to hang it on a wall and look at it for years. They deserve to know the line is real — who said it, when, in what room, answering what question. "Numbered and signed" has to apply to the words too.

So that's the house rule, starting now: no quote goes on Alleyoop work without a primary source. Where the trail goes cold, the quote stays off the wall.

The aggregators can keep the other nineteen.
